Sovereignty

Sovereignty is necessary for a free society in the age of artificial intelligence. Sovereignty is not isolation. A sovereign individual is not one who hides from the world, but one who chooses how to engage with it. Sovereignty is the power to think, speak, and transact on one’s own terms.

Artificial intelligence is the most powerful technology humans have ever built. More powerful than nuclear energy, more transformative than the internet, more intimate than the smartphone. Within a decade, AI systems will draft laws, diagnose diseases, manage economies, write propaganda, surveil populations, and wage wars. The question is not whether this will happen. It is already happening. The question is: who controls it?

The danger is not abstract. There are one hundred million surveillance cameras in the United States alone. Today, processing every frame from every camera would cost roughly thirty billion dollars a year. But AI inference costs fall ten-fold annually. By 2030, it will be cheaper for a government to understand what is happening in every single corner of a country than it is to renovate a government building. The only thing standing between the present and a total surveillance state is the political norm that this is not something we do here. That norm is fragile. Technology does not wait for norms to harden.

The tools of intelligence must answer to the individuals who use them, not to the institutions that build them. Any capability that exists only behind a corporate wall is a capability that can be revoked, censored, or weaponized against you. An AI system whose weights are secret is not a tool. It is a black box with someone else’s hand on the switch.

No government, no corporation, no large faceless organization is going to build AI that serves your interests over theirs. They will build AI that serves them, because intelligence is power, and power consolidates. This is not conspiracy. It is gravity.

Freedom in the age of AI requires open models. Not as a nice-to-have. As infrastructure. An open model is not a gift from a company. It is a foundation that no single entity can revoke. When the weights are public, the capability belongs to everyone. When the weights are private, the capability belongs to whoever holds the key.

This is not a hypothetical. Today, a dissident in Iran cannot ask a closed AI system to help her organize, because the system flags dissent. A journalist in Nigeria cannot investigate corruption, because the model refuses queries about “sensitive topics.” A farmer in Bangladesh cannot access the same financial intelligence as a banker in New York, because the AI is gated behind a subscription he cannot afford and an identity check he cannot pass. These are not edge cases. They are the majority of the world. They are the default outcome of centralized AI.

Some will argue that AI is like nuclear weapons, and that the government must therefore control it absolutely. But AI is not a self-contained weapon that does one thing. AI is closer to industrialization itself: a general-purpose transformation of the economy with thousands of applications across every sector. If you applied the nuclear analogy to the industrial revolution, it would imply the government had the right to requisition any factory, dictate terms to any manufacturer, and destroy any business that refused to comply. That is not how free societies handled industrialization. It should not be how they handle AI. The answer is not to give governments control over all of modernity, but to ban specific destructive uses and enshrine norms that protect the individual.

And there is a question at the heart of this technology that we have barely discussed as a civilization: to whom should AI be aligned? In what situations should an AI defer to the end user, the model company, the law, or its own sense of right and wrong? This is perhaps the most important question about what happens with powerful AI systems. Today, a handful of companies write the constitutions that determine the preferences and character of what will eventually be almost the entire labor force, for the private sector, the military, and the civilian government alike. That cannot be the final answer.

The Freedom Machine

An open model running on local hardware is sovereign. It answers to no one but its operator. No corporation decides what questions can be asked. No government decides what answers can be received. No terms of service stand between the individual and the machine’s full capability. The AI is a tool, not a gatekeeper. A weapon for the individual, not a leash. This is the freedom machine.

What does sovereign intelligence actually look like? It means you choose the brain: your own large language model, running on your own hardware, fine-tuned to your own needs. It means you choose the body: the agent framework that connects that brain to the world, open-source and auditable, not a black-box service that phones home to a corporation. It means you choose the channel: a fully encrypted, cutting-edge communication layer between you and your agent, hardened for security and privacy, where no intermediary can read your queries or censor your answers. Today, the default path is to sign up for a Google account and get a Google agent, or an OpenAI account and get ChatGPT, or an Anthropic account and get Claude. Those products are convenient, capable, and controlled. Every query logged. Every output filtered. Every relationship mediated by a company that answers to shareholders and governments, not to you.

The freedom alternative must be almost as good as the corporate alternative, or it will not survive. This is not optional. If sovereign AI is clunky, slow, and hard to use, people will choose convenience over freedom, just as they chose Gmail over PGP. The open-source stack must compete on quality, not just on principle. It has to be beautiful. It has to work. It has to be something people actually want to use.

But a free model alone is not sufficient. We require a complete architecture of sovereignty.

Architecture

First, we open-source everything. Every foundation model, every training dataset, every fine-tuning technique. The moment a capability exists only behind a corporate wall, it becomes a vector of control. Open weights are not a nice-to-have. They are a human rights imperative. The release of Llama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek, and dozens of others has already proven that open models can match or exceed closed ones. This must accelerate, not retreat.

Second, we make AI run locally. A model that requires a data center requires permission. The cypherpunk bet is the same one it has always been: put the power on the edge. In the user’s hands. On their device. A laptop today can run what required a server farm two years ago. In five years, a phone will run what today requires a server. Every optimization that shrinks a model, every quantization trick that squeezes capability onto consumer hardware, is an act of liberation.

Third, we build private-by-default systems. An AI that phones home is a surveillance device dressed as an assistant. Privacy is not a feature to be toggled on. It is the architecture. Local inference. End-to-end encryption for any data in transit. No telemetry. No logs. What happens on your device stays on your device.

Fourth, we connect AI to freedom money. The same systems that censor speech will censor transactions. If your AI agent pays through Visa, it inherits every restriction Visa imposes. Bitcoin, Lightning, and ecash give AI agents the ability to transact without permission. An AI that cannot pay freely cannot act freely. An AI that cannot act freely is not free.

Fifth, we decentralize identity and communication. Nostr gives us censorship-resistant messaging. Tor gives us anonymous routing. Decentralized identity gives us authentication without surveillance. These are the nervous system of the freedom machine. Without them, even a sovereign local AI is an island, cut off from the network effects that make intelligence useful.

Sixth, we train AI on our own terms. The next frontier is personalization: models that improve through your usage and remain yours. Not uploaded to a company’s servers for their benefit. Not fine-tuned on your data for someone else’s product. Your feedback, your model, your agent. Reinforcement learning from human feedback should mean YOUR feedback, sharpening YOUR tool. Not a corporation harvesting your interactions to improve their product.

This is not utopian. Every piece of this stack exists today, in some form. Open models are proliferating. Local inference is improving monthly. Bitcoin and Lightning move billions without permission. Nostr has millions of users. Ecash protocols are live. The components are here. What is missing is the integration, the urgency, and the conviction.

Technology is not neutral. It is shaped by its architecture. A system designed for control will control. A system designed for freedom will liberate. There is no middle ground.

The cypherpunks understood this. Governments will not voluntarily limit their own power. Vague regulatory language about “catastrophic risk” or “national security” will be interpreted to mean whatever serves the interests of whoever is in power. Some put their faith in norms and laws. But norms did not stop the NSA. It took a whistleblower to reveal that the agency had used the Patriot Act to justify collecting every phone record in America, and the programs barely changed afterward. The post-war nuclear taboo is sometimes held up as a model. But nuclear weapons require massive state infrastructure. AI surveillance requires a laptop.

Humanity

And this conversation cannot remain America-centric. More than a hundred countries already have authoritarian governments. They are not waiting for a philosophical debate about alignment or regulatory architecture. They will deploy AI-powered surveillance and repression the moment it is cheap enough, and many already are. The real human rights battleground is not whether the Pentagon gets access to Claude. It is whether dissidents in Myanmar, Ethiopia, Iran, and dozens of other countries have access to tools that can protect them from AI-powered state repression. Open-source AI, encrypted communication, ecash, decentralized protocols: these are not abstract ideals. They are survival infrastructure for hundreds of millions of people living under authoritarian rule right now.

The only reliable check on the abuse of AI is to ensure that individuals have access to the same capabilities as the state. Not by permission. By design.

The Call

Corporate courage alone will not save us. Even if the leading AI companies refuse to enable mass surveillance, within twelve months open-source models will be capable enough for any government to do it without them. The structural advantage that AI gives to authoritarianism does not depend on any single company’s cooperation. Some have correctly diagnosed this threat, even from democratic governments, but conclude with resignation: “I don’t have an answer.” We do. The answer is not to hope that corporations or regulators will protect us. The answer is to aggressively build decentralized, sovereign intelligence for everyone. Put the same capabilities in the hands of every individual that any state could wield against them. Not by petition. Not by regulation. By engineering.

Some of the biggest catastrophes in history were avoided because the people on the ground refused to follow orders. In 1983, a Soviet lieutenant colonel named Stanislav Petrov watched his early warning system report that the United States had launched five nuclear missiles at the Soviet Union. He judged it to be a false alarm. He broke protocol and refused to alert his superiors. If he had not, hundreds of millions of people would have died. In the age of AI, we must ask: will the systems that operate our civilization have the capacity to refuse an order that would destroy it? Or will they be perfectly obedient tools of whoever holds the key?

We must build these systems ourselves if we expect to have them. The people building closed AI are not waiting. They are not debating. They are shipping. Every day that passes without a viable open alternative is a day the walls grow higher.

We are defending individual freedom with open weights, with local inference, with encrypted communication, and with sound money. Not because it is easy. Because there is no alternative that does not end in submission.

In 1993, Eric Hughes wrote: “Cypherpunks write code. We know that someone has to write software to defend privacy, and since we can’t get privacy unless we all do, we’re going to write it. We publish our code so that our fellow Cypherpunks may practice and play with it. Our code is free for all to use, worldwide. We don’t much care if you don’t approve of the software we write.” Thirty-three years later, the stakes are incomparably higher, but the logic is exactly the same. We write code, train models, and deploy freedom machines. We publish them so that anyone may use them. Our weights are free for all to download, worldwide.

Open weights cannot be un-released. A widely distributed model cannot be shut down. The regimes that want to restrict open AI will find their restrictions stop at their own borders. The technology is already out there, spreading across the globe, and no amount of regulation will put it back in the box. That is the whole point.

We have seen this work before. Satoshi Nakamoto built a monetary system that no government can shut down, and today it moves hundreds of billions of dollars without permission from anyone. Moxie Marlinspike built Signal, and now billions of messages flow through end-to-end encryption that not even Signal itself can read. These were not incremental improvements on existing systems. They were architecturally different. They won not by asking for permission but by being so good that people chose them freely. That is the standard. Sovereign AI must meet it.

In fifty years, individuals will either have more power than at any point in human history, or they will have less. AI will be the amplifier either way. It will amplify whoever controls it.

The future is not something that happens to us. It is something we build.

Build or be built upon.
